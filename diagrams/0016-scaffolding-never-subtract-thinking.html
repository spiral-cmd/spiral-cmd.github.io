Scaffolding Should Never Subtract the Thinking from the Learner

Proficient Education, 2023 — When you help a student, the help should make them think more, not less.

What subtracting thinking looks like

Isaac is stuck. The tutor says:

"Try multiplying the two numbers."

The tutor identified the operation, made the strategic decision, and gave Isaac a step to execute. Isaac's job reduced to: do the multiplication I was told to do. The thinking was subtracted from him and done by the tutor instead. The problem feels solved. But next time Isaac is alone, he hasn't practised deciding which operation to use.

What preserving thinking looks like

Same situation, different response:

"What operation might connect these values?"

Isaac now has to: look at the numbers, consider possible operations, evaluate which one fits, and decide. The thinking stays with him. The tutor's role is to create the conditions for thinking, not to replace it.

⚠ The paradox

The tutor who gives more step-by-step help often feels more useful — the session is smoother, the student gets more answers right, progress looks faster. But that smoothness is the danger signal. If the student isn't struggling, they aren't building the mental muscles they'll need when the tutor isn't there. Productive struggle is the goal, not smooth progress.

✓ The test

After any interaction, ask: Did my prompt make the student think more, or less?

If they had to stop, consider, and decide before answering → good.
If they just executed what they were told → the thinking was subtracted.
