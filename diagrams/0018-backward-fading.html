Backward Fading

The most evidence-backed method for transitioning from guided to independent problem-solving. You fade support from the end of the solution process — the part the student is most ready to own — and work back toward the beginning.

The four stages

1
Complete the final step
Tutor shows the full solution except the last step. Isaac completes it.
"Here's the problem worked out — I've left the final calculation for you. What's the answer?"
↓
2
Complete the middle steps
Tutor shows the first step and the answer. Isaac fills in the middle.
"Here's how to start, and here's the answer. Can you work out what happens in between?"
↓
3
Complete from a prompt
Tutor gives only the starting strategy. Isaac produces the full solution.
"Try drawing a diagram first. Go from there."
↓
4
Independent
Isaac receives the problem cold. No prompts at all.
"Here's the problem. Go."

Why it works

Each stage removes one layer of support, but only one layer at a time. The student always has enough structure to succeed, but the amount they have to figure out for themselves increases gradually. It's called "backward" because you fade support from the end of the solution — the part the student is most ready to own — and work back toward the beginning, which is the hardest part to do alone.

In Isaac's context

If Isaac is used to co-solving, jumping straight to "here's a problem, solve it alone" will feel like being pushed off a cliff. Backward fading gives him a ramp over 6-8 weeks — each week the tutor does less, Isaac does more, and neither of them feels like the floor dropped out.
